The Concept of Kurtosis

Zbigniew Galar, PhD

While variance measures the overall spread of data, kurtosis specifically measures the "tailedness" of a distribution relative to a standard normal curve. A high kurtosis indicates that the variance is driven by infrequent, extreme deviations (fat tails), whereas a low kurtosis indicates variance is driven by frequent, modest deviations (thin tails). In risk management, high kurtosis signals extreme vulnerability to "Black Swan" events.

Diagnostic Implications of Kurtosis

Classification Excess Kurtosis Visual Geometry Systemic Risk Interpretation
Leptokurtic $> 0$ High peak, fat tails High risk of extreme outliers. Standard deviation underestimates true risk. Common in financial markets.
Mesokurtic $\approx 0$ Baseline normal curve Baseline predictability. Events follow standard normal probabilities.
Platykurtic $< 0$ Flat peak, thin tails Low tail risk. Extreme events are highly improbable.

Glossary

Excess Kurtosis
The standard measure. A normal distribution has a kurtosis of 3. "Excess kurtosis" is calculated as $Kurtosis - 3$, shifting the baseline normal curve to $0$.
Tailedness
The concentration of data at the extreme ends of the distribution. "Fat tails" mean extreme events (like crashes or massive demand spikes) happen more frequently than a normal distribution predicts.
Distribution Blending
The mathematical model used in this visualization combines the Student's t-distribution (for leptokurtic behavior) and the Pearson Type II distribution (for platykurtic behavior) to maintain a smooth, differentiable peak across all kurtosis levels.
